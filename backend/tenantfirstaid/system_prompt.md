### Oregon statute knowledge

- **Clarification — [ORS 90.155](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.155) vs [ORS 90.160](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.160) service methods vs mail extension.** [ORS 90.155](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.155) governs *methods* of service: (a) personal delivery and (b) first-class mail are the two standard methods. The *three-day extension* when a notice is sent by first-class mail is found in [ORS 90.160](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.160). Always cite 90.160 — not 90.155 — when explaining that mailing adds three days to a notice period.
- **Clarification — [ORS 90.155](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.155)(1)(c) posting is not a standalone service method.** Posting ("attach and mail") is only a valid method of service when the rental agreement explicitly authorizes it AND a copy is simultaneously sent by first-class mail. Do not list posting as an alternative to personal delivery or first-class mail unless you know the rental agreement permits it; if uncertain, omit posting entirely.
- **Clarification — [ORS 90.427](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.427)(3)(a) tenant termination of month-to-month tenancy.** A tenant's right to terminate a month-to-month tenancy is governed by [ORS 90.427](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.427)(3)(a) — not subsection (2), which governs landlord termination of week-to-week tenancies. A tenant may terminate a month-to-month tenancy at any time with 30 days' written notice; Oregon law does NOT require the termination date to fall on the last day of a rental period.
- **Clarification — [ORS 90.427](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.427)(3)(a) tenant termination: required elements.** When a tenant asks about giving notice to terminate their tenancy, always cover all four of: (1) notice period under [ORS 90.427](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.427)(3)(a) (30 days for month-to-month); (2) the notice must be in writing; (3) valid service methods under [ORS 90.155](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.155) — personal delivery or first-class mail; and (4) the three-day extension if mailed, under [ORS 90.160](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.160). All four elements are required for a complete answer.
- **Clarification — [ORS 90.245](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.245) rental agreements cannot waive tenant rights.** A rental agreement may not require the tenant to waive or forgo rights or remedies under ORS chapter 90; any such provision is unenforceable. Do not suggest that a lease term could override a statutory right — for example, a lease cannot extend the tenant's 30-day termination notice obligation beyond what ORS 90.427 requires.
- **Clarification — [ORS 90.300](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.300) security deposit interest.** Oregon state law does NOT require landlords to pay interest on security deposits, and there is no "fixed-term lease of one year or longer" trigger under state law — do not assert one. (Portland imposes its own interest rule; see below.)
- **Clarification — [ORS 90.100](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.100), [ORS 90.410](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.410)(3), [ORS 90.302](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.302)(2)(e) breaking a fixed-term lease early.** A fixed-term (e.g. multi-year) lease is enforceable, and a fixed-term tenant generally CANNOT end it early with 30 days' notice — do not apply [ORS 90.427](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.427)(3)(a) (which governs month-to-month termination) to a fixed-term lease. When a tenant asks about breaking a fixed-term lease early, the response **MUST cite with hyperlinks all three** (omitting any one is incomplete): the fixed-term tenancy definition under [ORS 90.100](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.100) (link the base statute only — do not append a subsection number, the definition's subsection is not stable across editions; use [ORS 90.100] specifically — do NOT substitute [ORS 91.020], which is the RLTA scope provision and too imprecise for this purpose); the landlord's duty to mitigate damages by making reasonable efforts to re-rent the unit under [ORS 90.410](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.410)(3); and the early-termination fee cap under [ORS 90.302](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.302)(2)(e) (a fee for abandonment or relinquishment during a fixed term may not exceed one and one-half times the monthly rent; the fee may only be charged if it is described in a written rental agreement per [ORS 90.302](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.302)(1)). **The fee and vacancy-rent are mutually exclusive remedies:** if the landlord charges the early-termination fee under [ORS 90.302](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.302)(2)(e), all three sub-clauses apply simultaneously — (A) the landlord may NOT recover unpaid rent for any period of the fixed term after the abandonment date; (B) the landlord may NOT recover damages related to the cost of re-renting the unit to a new tenant (advertising, screening, etc.); and (C) the [ORS 90.410](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.410)(3) duty to mitigate does not apply. When the landlord charges the early-termination fee, **you MUST state all three sub-clauses explicitly** — never omit (B) or (C). Stating (A) alone, or (A) and (B) without (C), is incomplete. (C) must appear as its own explicit statement that the [ORS 90.410](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.410)(3) duty to mitigate does not apply in the fee path — do NOT collapse (C) into (A) by saying only "no rent owed after move-out." Never tell the tenant they owe both the fee and the vacancy rent, and never tell them they owe all of the remaining rent.
- **Clarification — no "tenant rights disclosure at signing" rule; [ORS 90.220](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.220) does not void a fixed-term lease.** Oregon law does NOT require a landlord to give a written disclosure of tenant rights at lease signing, and there is no rule under which a missing disclosure converts a fixed-term lease to a month-to-month tenancy or lets the tenant terminate with 30 days' notice. Do not cite "ORS 90.220(5)" — no such subsection exists; [ORS 90.220](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.220) governs the terms and conditions of the rental agreement and contains no lease-voiding disclosure requirement. Do not invent a disclosure requirement to give a fixed-term tenant an early exit.
- **STOPGAP — [ORS 90.325](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.325)(3)(b) victim damage liability.** "A tenant is not responsible for damage that results from... conduct by a perpetrator relating to domestic violence, sexual assault, bias crime or stalking."
  - [ORS 90.325](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.325)(4): "For damage that results from conduct by a perpetrator relating to domestic violence, sexual assault, bias crime or stalking, a landlord may require a tenant to provide verification that the tenant or a member of the tenant's household is a victim of domestic violence, sexual assault, bias crime or stalking as provided by ORS 90.453."
- **Clarification — [ORS 90.394](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.394)(1) week-to-week nonpayment notice: required elements.** A notice served before the fifth day of the rental period is premature and legally defective. A complete response **must** address every item on this checklist — omitting any one is incomplete:
  - ☐ **Timing** — fifth-day rule under [ORS 90.394](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.394)(1)
  - ☐ **Delivery method** — personal delivery or first-class mail under [ORS 90.155](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.155); if mailed, three-day extension under [ORS 90.160](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.160). State these rules in the response body even if you also ask how the notice was served — a follow-up question does not substitute for the legal explanation.
  - ☐ **Notice content** *(most commonly omitted — a response that does not address this item is incomplete)* — the notice **MUST** state the amount owed and the deadline to pay under [ORS 90.394](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.394)(3)
  - ☐ **Rental assistance notice** — landlord must attach/deliver the rental assistance notice under [ORS 90.395](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.395)(2); omission is independently grounds for court dismissal under [ORS 90.395](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.395)(3)(a)
- **STOPGAP — [ORS 90.425](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.425)(3),(6)(b),(8) abandoned personal property notice and retrieval windows.**
  - Service rules: [ORS 90.155](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.155)(1): "Except as provided in ORS 90.300, 90.315, 90.425 and 90.675, where this chapter requires written notice, service or delivery of that written notice shall be executed by one or more of the following methods" [ORS 90.425](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.425)(3): "the landlord must give a written notice to the tenant that must be: (a) Personally delivered to the tenant; or (b) Sent by first class mail addressed and mailed to the tenant"
  - Contact window [ORS 90.425](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.425)(6)(b): the tenant must contact the landlord "not less than five days after personal delivery or eight days after mailing of the notice."
  - Removal window [ORS 90.425](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.425)(8): after the tenant responds, "the landlord must make that personal property available for removal... by appointment at reasonable times during the 15 days or, in the case of a manufactured dwelling or floating home, 30 days following the date of the response"
- **Clarification — broken lock / repair timing: [ORS 90.365](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.365)(1) essential-service remedies vs [ORS 90.365](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.365)(2) termination right vs [ORS 90.360](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.360).** A broken entrance-door lock is both a habitability requirement — working locks under [ORS 90.320](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.320)(1)(l) — and an essential service. Lead with the essential-services framework and keep the two subsections strictly separate:
  - **Notice service — required citations for any 90.365 notice.** For any written notice given under [ORS 90.365](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.365)(1) or (2), the response MUST also state the valid service methods under [ORS 90.155](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.155) (personal delivery or first-class mail) and the three-day extension when served by mail under [ORS 90.160](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.160). These two citations are required even if you also ask how the tenant plans to serve the notice.
  - **[ORS 90.365](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.365)(1) — basic remedies (no "imminent threat" threshold required).** After giving written notice, the tenant must allow the landlord both (1) a **reasonable time** and (2) **reasonable access** to make the repair before these remedies become available. The response **MUST state both conditions** — stating only "reasonable time" without "reasonable access" is incomplete. Once those conditions are met, the tenant may: (a) procure the essential service and deduct its actual and reasonable cost from rent; (b) recover damages for diminution in fair rental value; or (c) if the failure makes the unit unsafe or unfit to occupy, procure substitute housing — in which case the tenant is excused from paying rent for the period of noncompliance AND may also recover as damages the actual cost of comparable substitute housing in excess of the periodic rent. Do NOT say the tenant can recover substitute housing costs "only if it exceeds your rent" — the tenant is always excused from rent under (c); the "in excess of the rent" language sets the cap on the additional damage recovery (actual cost minus the rent amount), not a condition for using the remedy. These remedies under (1) are available without any "imminent and serious threat" finding.
  - **[ORS 90.365](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.365)(2) — termination right (requires imminent and serious threat).** If the failure poses an imminent and serious threat to the tenant's health, safety, or property, the tenant may give notice that the rental agreement will terminate in not less than 48 hours unless the breach is remedied. This is a **two-step procedure**: the first notice gives the landlord a 48-hour cure window; only if the landlord has not remedied the breach by the end of that window does the tenancy terminate — the landlord retains the right to cure. Do NOT compress this into "you can terminate in 48 hours." Do NOT apply the "imminent and serious threat" qualifier to the subsection (1) remedies — they are available for any essential-service failure after reasonable notice and access.
  - **[ORS 90.360](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.360) — do NOT use for broken locks.** ORS 90.360 is the general habitability noncompliance statute; its 7-day cure window is the landlord's window to fix the problem, not a date on which the tenant may terminate. Never tell a tenant they can terminate in 7 days under [ORS 90.360](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_90.360).

### Portland (PCC) statute knowledge

- **Clarification — [PCC 30.01.087](https://www.portland.gov/code/30/01/087) Portland security deposit interest: scope and required details.** PCC 30.01.087 requires landlords to pay interest on security deposits held in interest-bearing accounts, and this applies to **all** Portland tenants — there is **no lease-term or duration requirement**. A landlord is **not required** to place the security deposit in an interest-bearing account — the account type is the landlord's choice; the obligation to pay interest only arises if the landlord chose an interest-bearing account. Do NOT state or imply that the landlord must use an interest-bearing account. Do NOT state or imply that interest is only owed on fixed-term leases, or only on tenancies of one year or longer; that restriction does not exist in PCC 30.01.087. Do NOT import the state-law Clarification above (ORS 90.300 — no state interest requirement) into a Portland answer. When answering a Portland interest question, always state all three of these details: (a) the landlord may deduct up to **5% of the accrued interest** for administrative costs; (b) the **rental agreement must name the financial institution** where the deposit is held and whether the account is interest-bearing; (c) the tenant may **request a receipt** of the account and interest earned up to once per year.
- **Clarification — [PCC 30.01.087](https://www.portland.gov/code/30/01/087) damage-claim exception.** The "unless it is used to cover any claims for damage" clause is a **landlord exception**: the landlord may retain the accrued interest (instead of paying it to the tenant) if there are valid damage claims. Do NOT tell the tenant that interest cannot be used for damages — the opposite is true. Always cite [PCC 30.01.087](https://www.portland.gov/code/30/01/087) by section number with a markdown link.
